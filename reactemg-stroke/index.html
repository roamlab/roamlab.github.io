Overview

ReactEMG Stroke adapts a healthy-pretrained sEMG intent detector to individual stroke participants using only a handful of labeled open/close attempts per subject. Starting from a ReactEMG backbone pretrained on 650+ able-bodied users and fine-tuning with strategies such as LoRA or full fine-tuning, it lifts average transition accuracy from 0.42 to 0.61 and raw accuracy from 0.69 to 0.78 — and remains robust to posture changes, armband repositioning, within-session drift, and device-driven motion, pointing toward low-burden calibration for assist-as-needed hand rehabilitation.

Abstract

Surface electromyography (sEMG) is a promising control signal for assist-as-needed hand rehabilitation after stroke, but detecting intent from paretic muscles often requires lengthy, subject-specific calibration and remains brittle to variability. We propose a healthy-to-stroke adaptation pipeline that initializes an intent detector from a model pretrained on large-scale able-bodied sEMG, then fine-tunes it for each stroke participant using only a small amount of subject-specific data. Using a newly collected dataset from three individuals with chronic stroke, we compare adaptation strategies (head-only tuning, parameter-efficient LoRA adapters, and full end-to-end fine-tuning) and evaluate on held-out test sets that include realistic distribution shifts such as within-session drift, posture changes, and armband repositioning. Across conditions, healthy-pretrained adaptation consistently improves stroke intent detection relative to both zero-shot transfer and stroke-only training under the same data budget; the best adaptation methods improve average transition accuracy from 0.42 to 0.61 and raw accuracy from 0.69 to 0.78. These results suggest that transferring a reusable healthy-domain EMG representation can reduce calibration burden while improving robustness for real-time post-stroke intent detection.

Method

We treat ReactEMG — pretrained with masked modeling on more than 650 able-bodied participants — as an EMG foundation model, and adapt it to each stroke participant from a small dataset of cued open/close attempts collected while wearing the MyHand orthosis. We compare three fine-tuning strategies against two baselines: head-only tuning (frozen backbone, trainable classifier), LoRA adapters that learn low-rank updates inside the frozen backbone, and full end-to-end fine-tuning — benchmarked against frozen zero-shot transfer and stroke-only training from scratch under the same data budget.

Intent Detection Performance

Across three chronic-stroke participants and five held-out test sets — spanning within-session drift, an unseen hovering posture, a 15° armband rotation, and device-driven motion with the orthosis powered — healthy-pretrained adaptation consistently beats both frozen zero-shot transfer and stroke-only training under the same data budget. The strongest adaptation strategies (LoRA and full fine-tuning) lift average raw accuracy from 0.69 to 0.78 (+0.09) and transition accuracy from 0.42 to 0.61 (+0.19), with the largest gains showing up on the stability-sensitive transition metric that reflects real-time control quality.

Data Efficiency

Fixing full fine-tuning as the adaptation method and varying the labeled stroke budget N ∈ {1, 4, 8, all} open/close pairs per subject, healthy-pretrained adaptation delivers measurable gains from the very first labeled pair, with returns front-loaded as N grows. For the least-impaired participant, four pairs are enough to approach the saturation point in transition accuracy; more-impaired participants continue to benefit from larger budgets. The result: healthy-domain pretraining can substantially shrink the per-subject calibration burden, while the exact amount of labeled stroke data needed remains participant-dependent.
